    \section{Problem 1}
        You look through a camera toward an image of a hummingbird in a plane mirror. 
        The camera is 4.30 m in front of the mirror. 
        The bird is at camera level, 5.00 m to your right and 3.30 m from the mirror. 
        What is the distance between the camera and the apparent position of the bird's image in the mirror?

        \subsection{Solution}
            In a plane mirror, the object will appear as far from the mirror as it is legitimately. 
            Add the distance from the mirror of the camera to the bird's distance to the mirror.
            \begin{equation}
                \Delta x = 4.30\,\unit{\meter} + 3.30\,\unit{\meter} = 7.60\,\unit{\meter}
            \end{equation}

            Here use the Pythagorean theorem to find the distance.
            \begin{align}
                s   &=  \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2}
                    =   \sqrt{7.60^2 + 5.00^2}
                    =   \sqrt{57.76 + 25.00}
                    =   \sqrt{82.76}
                    =   \boxed{9.097\,\unit{\meter}}
            \end{align}

    \section{Problem 3}
        \begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.15\textwidth}
            \vspace{-30pt}
            \includegraphics[width=0.15\textwidth]{34-32.png} 
            % \label{fig:wrapfig}
        \end{wrapfigure}
        In Fig. 34-32, an isotropic point source of light $S$ is positioned at distance $d$ from a viewing screen $A$ and the light intensity $I_P$ at point $P$ (level with $S$) is measured. 
        Then a plane mirror $M$ is placed behind $S$ at distance $d$.
        By how much is $I_P$ multiplied by the presence of the mirror?

        \subsection{Solution}
            The equation for the intensity of light.
            \begin{equation}
                I = \frac{P_s}{4\pi r^2}
            \end{equation}

            This gives us an equation for $I_P$.
            \begin{equation}
                I_P = \frac{P_s}{4\pi d^2}
            \end{equation}

            Using this, we can create an equation for the intensity at point $P$ from teh light that reflected from the mirror (call it $I_M$).
            \begin{equation}
                I_M = \frac{P_s}{4\pi (3d)^2}
            \end{equation}

            We can use these to calculate the total intensity and relate that to $I_P$.
            \begin{align}
                I_{net} &=  I_P + I_M
                    =   \frac{P_s}{4\pi d^2} + \frac{P_s}{4\pi (3d)^2}\\
                    &=  \frac{P_s}{4\pi d^2} \left( 1 + \frac{1}{3^2} \right)
                    =   I_P \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)\\
                \frac{I_{net}}{I_P} &=  \boxed{\frac{10}{9} = 1.11}
            \end{align}

    \section{Problem 7}
        A concave shaving mirror has a radius of curvature of 35.0 cm.
        It is positioned so that the (upright) image of a man's face is 2.50 times the size of the face. 
        How far is the mirror from the face?

        \subsection{Solution}
            The upright image size difference tells us the magnification and solve for the position of the image ($i$).
            \begin{gather}
                m = -\frac{i}{p} = 2.50\\
                i = -2.50 p
            \end{gather}

            Usng this and the focal point, we can find a value of $p$.
            \begin{gather}
                \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{i} = \frac{2}{r}\\
                \frac{1}{p} - \frac{1}{2.50p} = \frac{2}{r}\\
                \frac{1}{p}\left( 1 - \frac{2}{5} \right) = \frac{2}{r}\\
                p   =   \frac{r}{2} \times \frac{3}{5}
                    =   \frac{3r}{10}
                    =   \frac{3}{10} \times 35.0\,\unit{\centi\meter}
                    =   \boxed{10.5\,\unit{\centi\meter}}
            \end{gather}

        \begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.25\textwidth}

        In Fig. 34-38, a beam of parallel light rays from a laser is incident on a solid transparent sphere of index of refraction n.
        (a) If a point image is produced at the back of the sphere, what is the index of refraction of the sphere?
        (b) What index of refraction, if any, will produce a point image at the center of the sphere?

        \subsection{Solution (a)}
            Assume that the index of refraction outside the sphere to be equal to 1.
            The source would have to be infinitely far away to produce parallel lines, so we can say that $p = \infty$ and $\underset{p \to \infty}{\lim} \frac{n_1}{p} = 0$.
            We have an equation that includes known information and our sought-out index of refraction of the sphere.
            \begin{equation}
                \frac{n_1}{p} + \frac{n_2}{i} = \frac{n_2}{i} = \frac{n_2 - 1}{r} = \frac{n_2}{r} - \frac{1}{r}
            \end{equation}

            The image hitting the other side at only one point means the image distance will be twice the radius.
            \begin{gather}
                \frac{n_2}{2r} =  \frac{n_2}{r} - \frac{1}{r}\\
                n_2 - \frac{n_2}{2} = \frac{n_2}{2} = 1\\
                n_2 = \boxed{2}
            \end{gather}

        \subsection{Solution (b)}
            This is similar, so we can do the same thing as we did for part (a).
            This time we just set $i = r$.
            \begin{gather}
                \frac{n_2}{r} =  \frac{n_2}{r} - \frac{1}{r}\\
                0   =   -\frac{1}{r} \to 0 = -1
            \end{gather}

            This makes it impossible.

    \section{Problem 41}
        A lens is made of glass having an index of refraction of 1.5. 
        One side of the lens is flat, and the other is convex with a radius of curvature of 20 cm. 
        (a) Find the focal length of the lens. 
        (b) If an object is placed 40 cm in front of the lens, where is the image?

        \subsection{Solution (a)}
            Use an equation for the focal length.
            \begin{gather}
                \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left( \frac{1}{r_1} - \frac{1}{r_2} \right)
            \end{gather}

            The value of $r_1$ will be $\infty$, while the value of $r_2$ will be $-20\,\unit{\centi\meter}$.
            We know that $n = 1.5$.
            \begin{align}
                \frac{1}{f} &=  (1.5 - 1)\left( 0 - \frac{1}{-20\,\unit{\centi\meter}} \right)
                    =   \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{20\,\unit{\centi\meter}}
                    =   \frac{1}{40\,\unit{\centi\meter}}\\
                f   &=  \boxed{40\,\unit{\centi\meter}}
            \end{align}

        \subsection{Solution (b)}
            Since it is at the focal point, the image will be \underline{infinitely far away}.

    \section{Problem 45}
        You produce an image of the Sun on a screen, using a thin lens whose focal length is 20.0 cm.
        What is the diameter of the image? 
        (See Appendix C for needed data on the Sun.)

        \subsection{Solution}
            The sun is about $1.5\E{11}\,\unit{\meter}$ away. 
            Its radius is about $6.96\E{8}\,\unit{\meter}$, leaving it with a diameter of $1.392\E{9}\,\unit{\meter}$.
            Our focal length is $0.200\,\unit{\meter}$.
            We can use this to find the image distance.
            \begin{gather}
                \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{i} = \frac{1}{f}\\
                \begin{align}
                    i   &=  \left( \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{p} \right)^{-1}
                        =   \left( \frac{1}{0.200\,\unit{\meter}} - \frac{1}{1.5\E{11}\,\unit{\meter}} \right)^{-1}\\
                        &\approx    \left( \frac{1}{0.200} \right)^{-1}
                        =   0.200\,\unit{\meter}
                \end{align}
            \end{gather}

            This can be used to find the magnification.
            \begin{align}
                m   &=  -\frac{i}{p}
                    =   -\frac{0.200\,\unit{\meter}}{1.5\E{11}\,\unit{\meter}}
                    =   -1.33\E{-12}
            \end{align}

            Multiply the diameter by the magnefication to find the image diameter.
            \begin{align}
                d'  &=  \left| md \right|
                    =   \left| -1.33\E{-12} \times 1.392\E{9}\,\unit{\meter} \right|\\
                    &=  \left| -\frac{29}{15625}\,\unit{\meter} \right|
                    =   \boxed{1.856\E{-3}\,\unit{\meter}}
            \end{align}

    \section{Problem 49}
        An illuminated slide is held 44 cm from a screen. 
        How far from the slide must a lens of focal length 11 cm be placed (between the slide and the screen) to form an image of the slide's picture on the screen?

        \subsection{Solution}
            Use the thin lens equation.
            \begin{equation}
                \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{i} = \frac{1}{f}
            \end{equation}

            There is also an equation that would allow for the lens to be between the slide and the screen.
            \begin{equation}
                p + i = 44\,\unit{\centi\meter}
            \end{equation}

            Combine these and solve for $p$.
            \begin{gather}
                i = 44\,\unit{\centi\meter} - p\\
                \frac{1}{11\,\unit{\centi\meter}} = \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{44\,\unit{\centi\meter} - p}
                    =   \frac{44\,\unit{\centi\meter} - p  + p}{p(44\,\unit{\centi\meter} - p)}\\
                44p - p^2 = 484\\
                0 = p^2 - 44p + 484\\
                p = \frac{44 \pm \sqrt{44^2 - 4 \times 484}}{2}
                    =   \boxed{22\,\unit{\centi\meter}}
            \end{gather}
